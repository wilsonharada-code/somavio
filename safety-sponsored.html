Bear country.
Know before you go.

What every visitor to Canmore and Kananaskis needs to know — before they hit the trails.

Updated June 2025
5 min read

Canmore and Kananaskis are home to one of the most intact mountain ecosystems in North America. That means wolves, cougars, elk and — most importantly — both black bears and grizzlies. Most encounters end without incident. The ones that don't almost always involve a visitor who wasn't prepared.

The wildlife you're likely to encounter

Grizzly bears are active throughout the Bow Valley from April through November. Black bears are present year-round at lower elevations. Elk are common in and around Canmore townsite — they look calm, but they account for more visitor injuries per year than bears do. Coyotes and wolves are present but rarely approached.

Parks Canada maintains a real-time wildlife sighting registry. SomaVio surfaces active alerts directly from that data — but conditions change faster than any platform can update. Always check posted signs at trailheads.

⚠ Active alert protocol
When SomaVio shows a wildlife alert for a specific area, treat it as current until Parks Canada clears it — even if the alert was issued hours ago. Bears move. A "clear" report from this morning may not reflect the situation at noon.

If you encounter a bear

Most bear encounters follow a pattern: you surprise the bear, the bear reacts. Your behaviour in the first five seconds determines what happens next.

1
Stay calm. Do not run.
Running activates the predatory response. Even a grizzly in full charge has been turned by bear spray. A person running is almost never stopped the same way.
2
Speak calmly. Identify yourself as human.
Talk in a low, firm voice. Wave your arms slowly. Most encounters at distance end here — the bear recognises you as human and moves off.
3
Back away slowly. Never turn your back.
Move sideways and diagonally. Keep facing the bear. Don't make direct eye contact but don't look away entirely. The goal is to increase distance without triggering movement.
4
Deploy bear spray if the bear charges and is within 10 metres.
Aim slightly downward and into the bear's path. A wide arc at face level. Research shows bear spray stops charges more reliably than firearms in surprise encounters. Practice removing the safety clip before you go — not during the encounter.

Bear spray — what it is and what it isn't

Bear spray is the single most effective deterrent available to non-rangers in the backcountry. It outperforms firearms in documented encounter studies. And yet the majority of international visitors arrive without it — often because they don't know it exists, or because they confuse it with pepper spray.

Legal status in Canada
Bear spray is legal to purchase and carry in Canada for wildlife deterrence. It is regulated as a pesticide, not a weapon.

Pepper spray is illegal in Canada when carried for use against people. It is classified as a prohibited weapon under the Criminal Code.

Do not bring bear spray from another country. It will be confiscated at the border. Purchase in Canmore before your hike — it's widely available and costs approximately CAD $40–60.

Bear spray cannot be carried on aircraft — in carry-on or checked baggage.
Before you go — the short checklist
✓  Carry bear spray and know how to use it
✓  Hike in groups of 4 or more when possible
✓  Make noise on the trail — talk, clap, use a bear bell
✓  Check the SomaVio safety bar and trailhead signs
✓  Tell someone your route and expected return time
✓  Download offline maps — signal is unreliable on most trails
✓  Emergency: 911 · Parks Canada 24/7: 1-888-773-8888
